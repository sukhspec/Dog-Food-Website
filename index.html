Safest Way to Switch Dog’s Food

Even after giving healthy meals, you may notice some uncommon behaviors in your dog, like refusing to eat, selective eating, indigestion, weight loss or gain, irritation, lack of energy, or chewing on inedible objects. All these symptoms show that you need to change your dog’s diet to include more healthy and nutritious meal options.

But before that, always consult a pet’s doctor or an expert for any unusual or different behavior of your fur baby.

Switching your dog's food may seem like a simple task, but it's important to do it gradually and safely to avoid upsetting yourdog's stomach and to ensure they receive the necessary nutrients. Here's a comprehensive guide on the safest way to switch your dog's food in a month:

Follow a week-by-week transition plan to switch your dog's food safely:

Week 1: Begin by mixing 75% of your dog's current food with 25% of the new food. This ratio helps your dog become accustomed to the new food without causing digestive upset.

Week 2: Adjust the ratio to 50% of each food – half of the old food and half of the new food.

Week 3: Increase the proportion of the new food to 75% while reducing the old food to 25%.

Week 4: By the fourth week, your dog should be fully transitioned to the new food, consuming 100% of the new food.

What Risky Ingredients Should You Avoid When Buying Dog Food?

Being paw parents, we always want to give the best to our fur babies. But something that seems good and healthy for us may cause serious health issues for our pets. Hence, you must be careful and have enough knowledge about the food and treats you serve them.


1. Artificial Preservatives:

Ingredients such as BHA (butylated hydroxyanisole), BHT (butylated hydroxytoluene), and ethoxyquin are artificial preservatives that have been linked to various health issues in dogs, including cancer and allergic reactions.  

2. Artificial Colors:

Synthetic colors like Red 40, Blue 2, and Yellow 5 are often added to dog food to make it more visually appealing. However, these artificial colors have no nutritional value and may cause allergic reactions or behavioral problems in some dogs.  

3. Artificial Flavors:

Artificial flavors are often used to enhance the taste of dog food, but they offer little to no nutritional benefit and may cause digestive upsetr allergic reactions in sensitive dogs.  

4. By-Products:

By-products are low-quality ingredients derived from animal parts that are not fit for human consumption, such as organs, beaks, feet, and feathers. They provide little nutritional value and may contain contaminants or toxins.  

5. Corn, Wheat, and Soy:

These are common allergens for dogs and are often used as cheap fillers in lower-quality dog foods. They offer limited nutritional value and can cause digestive issues, skin problems, and allergic reactions in sensitive dogs.  

6. Rendered Fat:

Rendered fat is a low-quality source of fatderived from the by-products of meat processing. It may contain toxins, heavy metals, or contaminants and can contribute to obesity and digestive problems in dogs.  

7. Meat Meal and By-Product Meal:

Meat meal and by-product meals are highly processed ingredients made from rendered animal parts and leftovers. They lack the quality and nutritional value of whole meats and may contain undesirable contaminants.  

8. Propylene Glycol:

This chemical is used as a preservative and flavor enhancer in some dog foods but has been associated with gastrointestinal upset, liver damage, and allergic reactions in dogs.  

9. Carrageenan:

Carrageenan is a thickening agent derived from seaweed that is often used in wet dog foods. It has been linked to digestive issues, inflammation, and even cancer in some studies.  

10. Propyl Gallate:

Another artificial preservative, propyl gallate, has been linked to health issues such as kidney and liver damage, as well as allergic reactions in dogs.

11. MSG (Monosodium Glutamate):

MSG is a flavor enhancer commonly used in human and pet foods. However, it has been associated with adverse reactions in some dogs, including nausea, vomiting, and neurological symptoms.  

12. Sugar and Corn Syrup:

Added sugars like corn syrup are often used to enhance the taste of dog food but provide no nutritional benefit and can contribute to obesity, diabetes, and dental problems in dogs.
